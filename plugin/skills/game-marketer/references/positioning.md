# Positioning

How the game shows up in the player's head when they encounter the marketing. Position once; everything (store page, trailer, ads, comms) descends from it.

## The five positioning slots

1. **The fantasy** — the role / feeling the player buys into. From `game-concept-creator`'s one-pager.
2. **The verb** — what the player does. The marketing line should embed at least one of the design's 3 verbs.
3. **The wedge** — the one different thing. From the concept one-pager. This is what *separates* the game from comp titles.
4. **The audience signal** — comp titles / aesthetic cues / cultural references that tell a player "this is for me."
5. **The proof** — gameplay footage that backs up the claims. Trailer + screenshots.

A successful piece of marketing hits all five. A piece that misses any one will underperform.

## Positioning line framework

```
For [audience], [game] is the [genre] where you [verb] to [goal] in [setting], delivering [aesthetic] through [wedge].
```

Examples:

- **Hades:** "For roguelike fans tired of repetitive runs, *Hades* is the action-roguelike where you fight your way out of the underworld with a different weapon each time, delivering tight combat through a unique narrative-progression mechanic."
- **Vampire Survivors:** "For action fans craving a chaos rush, *Vampire Survivors* is the auto-attack arena survival game where you mow down hordes to unlock chaotic builds, delivering a unique sense of mounting power through inverted-clicker mechanics."

Boil to one short sentence for the marketing line:

- *Hades:* "Die. Try again. Different weapons every time."
- *Vampire Survivors:* "What if Castlevania, but you don't move and you don't stop."

## Comp title strategy

Comp titles do work for you. They communicate genre, audience, and pacing in a name. Three rules:

1. **Pick comps the audience knows.** *Hollow Knight* communicates more to the audience than a niche reference.
2. **Comps signal both up and down.** "Hades-meets-Stardew" tells the player you're combining a hardcore action audience with a cozy audience. Pick comps that signal the audience you want.
3. **Don't claim equivalence.** "It's like *Hades* but better" oversells. "Inspired by *Hades* with a new fantasy" lands.

## Audience signals

Audiences self-identify with cultural cues. Some examples:

- **"Soulslike"** — high challenge, deliberate combat, deep lore, hardcore audience
- **"Cozy"** — low stress, beautiful, communal, casual-to-middle audience
- **"Roguelike"** — replayability, run-based, mechanically rich, mid-core
- **"Auto-battler"** — strategic, observation-heavy, depth-over-twitch
- **"Survivors-like"** — chaos, escalation, progression, accessible
- **"Metroidvania"** — exploration, ability-gating, atmospheric

Use the right cue. The wrong cue brings the wrong audience and hurts retention.

## Tone and voice

The marketing's tone should match the game's aesthetic:

- **Power fantasy:** confident, hyperbolic, cinematic
- **Cozy:** warm, lower-case-friendly, gentle, communal
- **Comedy:** self-aware, ironic, doesn't take itself seriously
- **Horror:** restrained, atmospheric, hint don't tell
- **Strategy:** measured, intelligent, respects player intellect
- **Web3 / collector:** authentic to the community, anti-bro voice, transparent

A Soulslike game with a TikTok comedy voice in marketing brings the wrong audience and harms retention. A cozy game with a power-fantasy trailer drives off the audience that would have loved it.

## Web3 positioning notes

Web3 games face a positioning trap: leading with "web3 / NFTs / earn" attracts speculators (who churn fast and pump-and-dump) and repels mass-market gamers. Lead with the *fantasy* and the *gameplay*; treat web3 as a feature mention, not the headline.

Exceptions:
- If the audience *is* the web3 community (collector/trader fantasy), lead with web3 elements
- Mint events specifically need web3-forward comms (because the audience is web3-native at that moment)

## Multi-platform positioning

A game shipping on PC, mobile, and web has the same fantasy — but the positioning emphasis shifts:

- **Steam:** depth, hours, replayability; comp titles in the genre; community signals (Discord, mods)
- **App Store / Google Play:** quick onboarding, session length, "play in a coffee break"; comp titles in mobile category
- **Web:** instant play, no install, free trial; lead with low-friction
- **Console:** premium feel, cinematic, comfortable on TV

Same fantasy, different framing per platform.

## Anti-patterns

- **Generic "epic adventure"** — meaningless; doesn't position
- **Feature-list marketing** — "47 ships, 200 quests, 5 factions" — features without fantasy
- **Comp-title overstatement** — "the next *Zelda*"
- **Tone mismatch** — slick AAA-style trailer for an indie cozy game (or vice versa)
- **Web3-as-headline** for a mass-market game (turns off the mass market)
- **Position by negation** — "not like other games" — players want to know what it *is*, not what it isn't

## Output

Capture in the marketing brief:
- The 5 positioning slots filled
- The one-line marketing line (≤15 words)
- The expanded positioning paragraph
- The 2–3 comp titles and what each comp signals
- The chosen tone and voice
- Per-platform variants if multi-platform
